Who Does AI Safety Leave Out?

Cultural misalignment is a catastrophic risk vector, not a fairness footnote.

I started looking into AI safety expecting to find a field. What I found was closer to a conversation: ongoing, contested, and largely happening without me.

The more I read, the more one question surfaced. What does "safety" actually mean here? The honest answer is that nobody fully agrees. There are researchers, manifestos, organisations, and frameworks, each pulling in different directions. There are brilliant people scattered across the world working on this in fragments, each from their own tradition, their own context, their own understanding of what is at stake. And beneath all of it sits a question the field rarely asks directly: safe for whom?

That question is personal for me. I come from a community that does not feature prominently in the literature. We are not the assumed user, not the imagined beneficiary, not the culture whose values get encoded into the default. And so I find myself asking something that sounds simple but runs very deep: will AI ever actually serve my community?

I do not think that question is separate from the technical work of safety. I think it is the technical work of safety. The field just has not caught up to that yet.

The Geography of AI Safety

AI safety research is concentrated. A handful of organisations, almost all based in the United States and United Kingdom, staffed overwhelmingly by people from WEIRD societies (Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, Democratic), are currently setting the terms of the global conversation about how AI should behave.

This is not a moral accusation. Many of these researchers are brilliant, careful, and genuinely motivated by concern for humanity as a whole. But good intentions do not dissolve the epistemological problem: you cannot fully anticipate the failure modes of a system you are building for people whose values, contexts, and ways of knowing you do not share.

The training data tells the story plainly. Large language models are trained predominantly on English-language text, weighted heavily towards Western sources and produced within particular ideological frameworks (see, e.g., Bender et al., 2021; Dodge et al., 2021). The resulting systems carry assumptions about individualism, legal norms, and what "polite" or "helpful" or "honest" means that are not culturally universal. They feel natural to the people who built them. That is precisely what makes them easy to miss.

"Safety" is Not What You Think It Is

Here is something the field rarely admits openly: there is no settled definition of AI safety.

Ask ten researchers and you will get ten answers. Some mean robustness: systems that do not break in unexpected ways. Some mean alignment: systems that pursue the goals we intend. Some mean governance: institutional structures that keep powerful AI accountable. Some are thinking about existential risk decades from now. Others are thinking about the harm a biased hiring algorithm does this afternoon.

These are not the same problem. They do not share the same methods, the same language, or even the same underlying values about what a good outcome looks like. The field is, in many ways, still negotiating with itself about what it is trying to do.

For communities outside the Western, anglophone core of that negotiation, this ambiguity is not abstract. It means there is no clear door to knock on. No obvious way to say: this is what safety needs to mean for us, in our context, given our history with institutions that claimed to act in our interests. The conversation is already underway, the terms are already being set, and most of the world is watching from outside the room.

That is not an accident. It is a structural feature of how the field developed. And it has consequences.

Alignment for Whom?

The technical alignment problem, as conventionally framed, asks: how do we ensure that an AI system reliably pursues the goals we intend it to pursue? The implicit assumption is that "we" can be specified, that our goals are coherent and legible and representable in a training objective.

They cannot. Not because human values are hopelessly complex, though they are, but because which humans we are aggregating across changes the answer substantially.

Consider something as basic as the relationship between individual autonomy and collective obligation. In much of the Western liberal tradition, protecting individual rights sits close to a foundational axiom. Across many East Asian, African, and Indigenous societies, the relationship between self and community is structured very differently. Neither view is wrong. They are different moral grammars. An AI system trained to optimise for one will systematically fail, or actively harm, people operating within the other.

This is not a hypothetical. It is a structural feature of the current approach. And as AI moves from consumer applications into healthcare, education, law, governance, and economic infrastructure, the stakes grow accordingly.

The Lock-in Problem

AI safety researchers rightly worry about value lock-in: the possibility that a sufficiently powerful system could freeze a particular set of values into the fabric of civilisation, foreclosing future moral growth or correction. It is one of the most serious long-run risks on the table.

The irony is that a version of this risk is already materialising, not through misaligned superintelligence, but through the mundane, accelerating rollout of present-day systems built on a narrow value base.

When AI becomes infrastructure, when it mediates hiring, credit, medical diagnosis, legal aid, content moderation, and public services at scale, the assumptions baked into it become structural. They do not announce themselves as cultural preferences. They present as defaults, as neutral ground, as simply how things work. Communities whose values sit outside that encoded default are not consulted. They are processed.

This is cultural misalignment as risk, not grievance. The concern is not only that some groups are treated unfairly, though they are. The concern is that the systems are wrong: optimising for a partial, misspecified model of human flourishing, and by the time this is widely recognised, the architecture will be too entrenched to easily fix.

Safety's Blind Spot is Epistemic

There is a second-order problem here. It is not only that AI systems are built on narrow values. It is that the field of AI safety itself has a narrow epistemology: a particular way of framing which problems matter, what counts as evidence, which methodologies are credible.

The dominant paradigms emerged from specific intellectual traditions, analytic philosophy, theoretical computer science, economic decision theory, and a version of consequentialism shaped largely by Anglocentric institutions. These are serious traditions. They are not the only ones.

Indigenous knowledge systems, African philosophy, South Asian ethical frameworks, and other non-Western traditions have developed rich accounts of how intelligent systems, including social and institutional ones, should remain accountable, reversible, and embedded within communities rather than imposed upon them. These traditions are rarely cited in alignment research. They are almost never used to generate safety requirements.

This is an epistemic loss. It is also a risk factor. Safety research that cannot draw on the full range of human moral intelligence is working with incomplete tools. The failure modes it cannot conceptualise are the ones most likely to surprise it.

Not Diversity Theatre — Epistemic Plurality

The response to these concerns often defaults to a familiar register: representation, inclusion, diverse hiring. These matter. They are not sufficient.

Diversity of faces without diversity of frameworks changes the optics without changing the epistemics. A researcher from the Global South trained entirely within Western AI safety paradigms, at Western institutions, citing Western literature, is unlikely to import significantly different assumptions. Not because of any personal failing, but because the system of knowledge production has done its work.

What is required is something harder: genuine engagement with non-Western intellectual traditions as sources of insight, not merely as communities of users to accommodate. It means reconsidering which questions the field is asking, not only who is asking them. It means treating cultural misalignment not as downstream of "real" safety work, but as constitutive of it.

The Stakes Are Not Abstract

Let me be direct about what is at risk.

If AI systems continue to scale in capability and reach while remaining culturally misaligned with the majority of the world's population, several outcomes become more likely. Systems will fail across healthcare, justice, and welfare in ways that fall disproportionately on communities already least equipped to push back. Values will be encoded into global infrastructure without consent from those most affected. And the feedback loops that might correct these failures, local accountability, cultural friction, community resistance, will be progressively weakened as AI becomes more capable and more deeply embedded.

None of this requires a science fiction scenario. It is the trajectory implied by current practice.

Meanwhile, the legitimacy of AI safety as a field depends on its claim to be concerned with humanity's future, all of it. That claim is hard to sustain when the field's outputs systematically reflect the priorities of a small, wealthy fraction of that humanity. Scepticism of AI governance, particularly in the Global South, is growing. That scepticism is not irrational. It is a reasonable response to a field that talks about human values while demonstrating, in practice, whose values it actually centres.

Will AI Ever Serve My Community?

I keep coming back to that question. Not as rhetoric, but as a genuine and open one.

The honest answer is: not automatically. Not by default. Not as a side effect of building systems optimised for someone else's context and hoping they translate.

But the question is worth asking loudly, because it exposes something the field tends to obscure behind technical language. "Alignment," "safety," "benefit to humanity" are phrases that carry enormous moral weight. They imply a universality that the current work does not yet earn. Claiming to build AI that is safe for humanity while designing it around a fraction of humanity's values is not a technical limitation to be solved later. It is a foundational error in the present.

The argument here is not about charity or inclusion. It is about rigour. A safety field that leaves out most of the world is not a global safety field. It is a local one with global reach, and that combination, at the scale AI is moving towards, is its own kind of catastrophic risk.

Whose safety are we talking about? That question does not have a clean technical answer. It has a political one. And until the field is willing to sit with the discomfort of that, the conversation about AI safety will remain exactly what I found when I first went looking:

Almost there. But not quite for me.
